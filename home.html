FOCAL

FOrmal CinemAtics ontoLogy

FOCAL (FOrmal CinemAtics ontoLogy) conceptualises and formalises movies' content, in a format which machines can easily understand. In order to achieve this goal, it proposes an ontology, an explicit specification tool that models a domain of knowledge by means of its three representational primitives: classes (sets, collections, or kind of things), attributes (characteristics, aspects or parameters that things can have), and properties (relations among class members).

The proposed ontology is a semantically rich, machine-interoperable knowledge graph that comprehensively describes a movie in terms of its content (scenes) and metadata (description, actors, etc...). The semantic-based representation of a movie, with a vocabulary to model the overall properties of its structural and content information, is also interlinked with other standard and existing ontologies. Leveraging on Linked Data best practices, it is thus possible to perform queries and reasoning with other ontologies in order to infer new knowledge.

The main goal of this work is to enable the interrogation of the inside of a video and to provide viewers with new and enhanced searching opportunities, such as "find me a movie scene where there is a caucasian guy hanging onto an airplane where the movie production took place in Europe".

Why movies?

As an increasing number of movies is being produced every year, there is an ever growing need to capture the content of videos in order to make them searchable and machine-readable.

This would potentially unlock a various set of applications from automatic video summarisation, to personalization systems being able to recommend particular movies or even movie scenes (especially from the big streaming services such as Netflix, Hulu, Amazon Prime, Disney plus etc.), to exploration of objects within the movies. As for the last one, given the wealth of streams available in video content and exploiting the benefits of the Web of Data, the potential for supporting such queries is very high.

Criteria

In order to realise the goal of achieving a comprehensive knowledge graph that describes movies and their contents, the ontology is based on the following criteria:

  • Extensible. The schema should be abstract enough to provide margin for supporting future evolution of multimedia standards.
  • Interoperable. The schema should be defined in a standardised interoperable format, thus ensuring that the schema can be used for different tasks, in different systems and by multiple agents.
  • Standardised. The schema should reuse and hence align with various standards, ontologies or other specifications.
  • Movie scene description. The ontology should be able to represent movie content. In other words, it should be a bridge between the multimedia signals in movies and a higherlevel abstract semantic annotation. Therefore, the ontology should be able to make multimedia content understandable by the machine as a user might understand it.
  • Metadata knowledge. Apart from scene concepts, our schema should describe movie metadata, for example actors, genre, and so on.

Interlinking

Many potential well-known knowledge graphs could be useful for Focal ontology to interlink to, hence obtaining more information on the annotated movies. Five ontologies enriched this projects linking its content to other resources on the Web of Data:

  • Ontology for Media Resources This is an ontology for describing media resources on the web. (Namespace: http://www. w3.org/ns/ma-ont#).
  • Simple Knowledge Organization System (SKOS) This is a W3C recommendation designed for representation of thesauri, classification schemes, taxonomies, subject-heading systems, or any other type of structured controlled vocabulary. (Namespace: http://www.w3.org/2004/02/skos/core#).
  • Friend of a Friend (FOAF) This is a computer language defining a dictionary of people-related terms that can be used in structured data. (Namespace: http://xmlns.com/foaf/0.1/).
  • Dublin Core (DC) It is a set of fifteen "core" elements (properties) for describing resources, that can be digital (video, images, web pages, etc.) as well as physical such as books or works of art. (Namespace: http://purl.org/dc/elements/1.1/).
  • Onyx Emotion Ontology This is an ontology designed to annotate and describe the emotions expressed by user-generated content on the web or in particular Information Systems. (Namespace: http://www.gsi.dit.upm.es/ontologies/onyx/ns#).

Interlinking to external sources makes the knowledge graph semantically richer with external information impossible to get from the movie content or movie metadata alone. For instance, let us consider Christopher Nolan's 'Memento', where Guy Pearce plays the leading role. The movie’s metadata will only provide such link (i.e. between actor and fictional character), however, interlinking the Guy Pearce entity with its equivalent in an external source, will give more information such as the gender, date of birth, links to their parent’s entity, etc.

Data

A significant challenge lies in building a bridge between multimedia content and the Semantic Web: the black-box nature of the media.

Throughout the years, several attempts have been made in order to "unbox" it: Nicolaescu and Klamma [1] created a Web-based system to help user semantically annotate videos, Haas et al. [2] developed a platform for the identification of intelligent media content through the engagement of the end-audience, and LinkedTV D2.2 [3] provides a system that handles video analysis through interactive and informative services.

In this project, the data (content and metadata) has been manually inserted, thus there is no exploration on how to harvest it from multimedia. Other projects instead have enriched their systems with automatic extraction, such as the one by Chortaras et al. [4], with state-of-the-art visual content analysis techniques, Dasiopoulou et al. [5], with F-logic rules to map video signals into ontological concepts, and Orlandi et al. [6] with video processing algorithms that extract multimedia content used to create a semantic knowledge graph.

Case study: Memento

The case study for the application of Focal is Memento (2001), a movie written and directed by Christopher Nolan.

In addition to having received numerous accolades, including Oscar nominations for Best Original Screenplay and Best Film Editing, in 2017 it was deemed by the United States Library of Congress "culturally, historically, or aesthetically significant" and selected for preservation in the National Film Registry.

Complex and absorbing thriller, Memento has an intriguing structure and deep meditations on the nature of perception and life: in the case of Focal, it was a perfect application because it is presented as two different sequences of scenes interspersed during the film:

  • The first one is a series in black-and-white that is shown chronologically,
  • and the other one is a series of color sequences shown in reverse order.
Eventually, the two sequences meet at the end of the film, producing one complete and cohesive narrative.

Thanks to this structure, unique in the history of cinema, it was possible to overcome a great challenge in movies' content: the question "what signals the start of a new scene?", since every new scene is clearly identified by a change in color. Moreover, Memento plays heavily with the narrative construction, i.e. the structural framework that underlies the order and manner in which a narrative is presented to a reader, listener or, in this case, viewer.

Since time is the fundamental dimension of a film's content and Focal deals with the representation of dynamic aspects of cinema, Memento is the best case study to carry out an in-depth analysis on the distinction between fabula (the chronological order of the events contained in the story) and syuzhet (the linear progression of narrative from beginning to end), as described by the Russian Formalists Victor Shklovsky and Vladimir Propp [7].

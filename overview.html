OveRWieW

Focal ontology aims to semantically crystallize the dynamic content of a movie, interlinking its components in a dense web of meanings and relationships. Following the guidelines of Protégé ontology software, I analyzed all the main ways in which a Movie can be described, by providing categories (classes) and binary relations between objects of the categories (individuals) or between an object and a data value (literals). In the first case the relationships are called object properties and in the second case they are known as data properties.

The ontology can be downloaded in three formats: RDF/XML, JSON, or Turtle syntax.

Since Focal not only aims to investigate the film content, but also the metadata of the film itself (i.e. the data providing information about one or more aspects of the data), two major areas of entities have been identified to describe the main class Movie:

  • relations between the Movie and the real world;
  • relations between the Movie and its content;
  • (and, of course, the metadata of the movie);

Between Movie and real world

Relations with the outside world have been designed to connect the Movie with cardinal classes:

  • the place where the film was produced, described by means of DBPedia, with the class dbo:Country;
  • the awards the film has won or was nominated for (Oscar, Golden Globe, Academy Awards, BAFTA, etc.), with focal:Award;
  • the production company of the movie (e.g. Universal Pictures, Paramount, etc.), with dbo:Production_Company
  • the production company of the movie (e.g. Universal Pictures, Paramount, etc.), with dbo:Production_Company
  • the main people who contributed to the making of the film, with dbo: Person containing the following subclasses: dbo:Film_Director, dbo:Musical_Artist, dbo:Actor, dbo:Writer;
  • the genre of the movie, described by means of 68 different categories, with dbo:Genre. Take a look at them by clicking on the button below:

Genres

Moreover, data properties belonging to focal, to skos and to ma describe the metadata of the Movie and further connections between it and the real world:

  •  ma:title , a data property that connects the movie to its title and allows a xsd:string literal;
  •  ma:identifier , a URI identifying a media resource. It allows a xsd:anyURI literal;
  •  ma:language , a data property that connects the movie to its language and allows a xsd:string literal;
  •  skos:isBasedOn , a resource from which this movie is derived (can be a book, a tale, a novel etc.). It allows a xsd:string literal;
  •  focal:is_inspired_by , a resource from which this movie is inspired (can be a book, a tale, a novel etc.). It allows a xsd:string literal;
  •  focal:box_office , that allows a xsd:integer literal;
  •  skos:keywords , a data property that connects the movie to a list of keywords, separated by comma, which describe the content of the movie. It allows a xsd:string literal;
  •  skos:about , similar to the previous one, but this refers to the content / plot of the movie. It allows a xsd:string literal;
  •  skos:runtime , that allows a xsd:integer literal;
  •  skos:releaseDate , a data property that connects the movie to its release date, expressed in YYYY-MM-DD. It allows a xsd:date literal;
  •  focal:imdb_rating , that allows a xsd:double literal;
  •  ma:frameSize , a data property that defines the frame size of the resource (e.g., width and height of 720 and 480 units, respectively). It allows a xsd:string literal;

Between Movie and its content

As may be seen, many of the classes that have been used for real-world entities connected to movies (e.g. dbo:Production_Company, dbo:Genre) come from important and already established ontologies. In fact, the purpose of Focal is mainly concentrated in the content of the films, where almost all the classes and properties have been thought of ex novo.

  • focal:Sequence: the most important subclass of ‘Movie’, divided in focal:Scene and focal:Shot, where Sequence is a number of scenes connected through a common storyline, Scene is a unit of story that takes place at a specific location and time, and Shot is the most basic unit of a film, a single continuous angle of view taken by a camera;
  • focal:Setting: the locations, the settings of the happenings in the Movie;
  • focal:Character: the characters in the Movie through which the story is conveyed, divided in Background_Character and Protagonist;
  • focal:Screenplay: every instance of this class is a Line from the script of the Movie. This line is connected to the character who says it, to the one who hears it and it’s described with several metadata to enrich its meaning and scope, such as  focal:dialog_style  (that can be, among the others, “voice_over", “pre_lap", “on_radio", etc.) or  focal:is_a_lie ,  focal:is_ironic ,  focal:is_metaphor_for , etc.;
  • focal:Significant_Thing: key objects which form an important part of the story, e.g. a gun, a car, a clock;
  • focal:Action: actions and states which set the base for the progress of the story in a movie like death, murder, chase, etc.
  • focal:Emotion: the emotions felt by the characters, divided in focal:Basic_Emotion (anger, anticipation, disgust, fear, joy, sadness, surprise, trust), focal:Mild_Emotion (acceptance, annoyance, apprehension, boredom, distraction, interest, pensiveness, serenity) and focal:Intense_Emotion (admiration, amazement, ectasy, grief, loathing, rage, terror, vigilance), according to Robert Plutchik's wheel of emotions in “A general psychoevolutionary theory of emotion", 1980;

Wheel of emotions

  • focal:Sound: what can be heard during the vision of the Movie, divided in focal:Music, the tracks and soundtrack, and focal:Soundscape, the "noise", sound or combination of sounds that forms or arises from an immersive environment (chirping of birds, cars, barking of dogs, etc.).

Through Focal ontology, each of the aforementioned classes is connected to its sisters in a dense network of relationships that ensure that every moment of the film the viewer knows exactly what is happening, at what time of day, in a real or fictional, natural or artificial setting, who are the characters involved, what emotions are they feeling and with what intensity, what music is used to convey those emotions and actions, what words are they saying and with what meaning (for example, of manipulation, incitement to a victory, love courtship) , what objects appear and to whom they belong, who created them or what their purpose is, etc.

Among this information, the most interesting is that that can be deduced from the classification of Sequence, Scene and Shot.

Sequence, Scene, Shot

When it comes to the content of movies, apart from the aforementioned eight ontological categories (Sequence, Character, Screenplay, Setting, Significant_Thing, Action, Emotion, Sound) there are many other characteristics speciﬁc to a movie like cinematography, editing, background music, special effects, etc. which form the expressive and aesthetic content of a movie. These characteristics are manifested through a Sequence, Scene or Shot and thus are modeled as their attributes. Below, there is the complete list of these 'data properties':

  •  focal:starts_at : it defines the starting time ('hh:mm:ss') of a Scene, Sequence or Shot. It allows a xsd:time literal;
  •  focal:ends_at : it defines the ending time ('hh:mm:ss') of a Scene, Sequence or Shot. It allows a xsd:time literal;
  •  focal:diegetic_level : it relates a Shot, Scene or Sequence to its diegetic level. See more details about what actually is a diegetic level in the panel below, An ontology about TIME. The Level of the story contained in the Movie is 0. No. 1 means that it is a story within the movie, told by a character. If in the story, a new character starts telling another story, this latter one will have no. 2, and so on. In the case of a "breaking of the fourth wall", the Level shall be -1. It allows a xsd:integer literal;
  •  focal:temporal_manipulation : It relates the Sequence, Scene or Shot to its temporal manipulation, or retiming effect. It allows a xsd:{"fast motion" , "freeze" , "loop" , "reverse motion" , "slow motion"} literal;
  •  focal:opening_credits : it indicates if the Sequence contains opening credits (true / false). It allows a xsd:boolean literal;
  •  focal:closing_credits : it indicates if the Sequence contains opening credits (true / false). It allows a xsd:boolean literal;
  •  focal:special_effects : it indicates if the Sequence, Scene or Shot contains special effects. It allows a xsd:boolean literal;
  •  focal:intensity : it indicates the intensity of the Sequence, Scene or Shot, from 1 to 10. It allows a xsd:double literal;
  •  focal:color : this data property allows you to define if a Sequence, Scene or Shot is in color (if True) or black and white (if False). It allows a xsd:boolean literal;
  •  focal:type : it associates the Sequence, Scene or Shot to particular types of narration. It allows a xsd:{"dream" , "hallucination" , "nightmare" , "story", "memory"} literal;
  •  focal:sets_during : it relates the Sequence, Scene or Shot to the moment of the day in which it is set. It allows a xsd:{"dawn" , "day" , "dusk" , "evening" , "morning" , "night"} literal;
  •  focal: : it relates the Sequence, Scene or Shot to the moment of the day in which it is set. It allows a xsd:{"dawn" , "day" , "dusk" , "evening" , "morning" , "night"} literal;
  •  focal:weather : it relates the Sequence, Scene or Shot to its weather. It allows a xsd:{"cloudy" , "rainy" , "snowy" , "stormy" , "sunny" , "windy"} literal;
  •  focal:mood : it describes a Sequence, Scene or Shot in a subjective manner (e.g. scary, funny). It allows a xsd:string literal;

Some properties belong exclusively to the class Scene:

  • owl:sameAs: this is an object proerty, not a data property. When used for Scene, it means that there is a Scene that has been shown before or will be shown after: this is frequent in the case of the movie Memento, where there are scenes that are shown one time to the viewer (cue scenes) and then again (key scene) to help him memorize and reconstruct the flow of the story. Basically, these scenes are exactly the same ones, just shown two times. Its Range is Scene;
  •  focal:fabula : it associates the Scene to an integer > 0, according to its chronological position in the order the movie. See more details in the panel below, An ontology about TIME. This property allows a xsd:integer literal;
  •  focal:flashback : it defines if the Scene is a flashback. Thanks to the property fabula, it's also chronologically reordered inside the story. It allows a xsd:boolean literal;
  •  focal:flashforward : it defines if the Scene is a flashforward. Thanks to the property fabula, it's also chronologically reordered inside the story. It allows a xsd:boolean literal;

Some properties belong exclusively to the class Shot:

  •  focal:framing : it is the placement and position of the subjects in a Shot. It allows a xsd:{"group shot" , "insert shot" , "over-the-hip shot" , "over-the-shoulder shot" , "point-of-view shot" , "single shot" , "two shot"} literal;
  •  focal:size : it is how much of the setting or subject is displayed within a given frame of a Shot. It allows a xsd:{"close-up" , "extreme close-up" , "full shot" , "long shot" , "medium shot"} literal;
  •  focal:transition : the technique used in the post-production process of film editing by which Shots are combined. It allows a xsd:{"cut to" , "dissolve to" , "fade to" , "jump cut to" , "match cut to" , "smash cut to" , "wipe to"} literal;

An ontology About Time

Time passing is an opportune way to enhance the film’s wow-factor and showcase the creativity of the director or the editor during post-production; there are myriad ways in which films do, in fact, represent time. this is due to the fact that films (along with books, oral stories and other storytelling methods) are perfect ways to employ non-linear narrative. Known also as disjointed narrative or disrupted narrative, the non-linear narrative is a narrative technique where events are portrayed out of chronological order or in other ways where the narrative does not follow the direct causality pattern of the events featured, such as parallel distinctive plot lines, dream immersions or narrating another story inside the main plot-line.

Which are the reasons why non-linear narrative is employed? Some television series use nonlinear narrative in the beginning of a season or episode as a narrative hook, showing an intense or shocking event, and then extensively explore the past and the reasons that lead that event to happen. However, we must remember that humans are naturally and irresistibly drawn to narrative and to attract attention and create expectation in their audience: this is why non-linear narrative is something deeply rooted inside our communication skill.

Films such as Memento are commonly referred to as popular, successful examples of non-linear screen storytelling. In fact, the story in the movie is quite strongly linear; each scene contains information that adds to what has previously been revealed to the audience. However, many sequences of the movie are shown in a non-chronological way. Director Christopher Nolan chose this fashion to invite the public to empathize with the movie and its protagonist, allowing the viewers to experience what it feels to have a retrograde amnesia – to know only what is in the present without a past to account for.

The protagonist, Leonard Shelby, in fact, suffers from this condition that incapacitates him to form new memories after a terrible incident where his wife was raped by an unknown assailant. The narrative of the film is thus largely predicated on the medical condition of Leonard and his (and our, as viewers) inability to discern and judge aptly.

In the film, color sequences are alternated with black and white sequences.

  • black and white sequences are put together in chronological order;
  • color sequences are shown in reverse order.
Interestingly, Nolan beautifully weaved a story that goes back as it develops. The black and white sequences are very objective, while the color sequences are subjective. The color sequences always start with Leonard’s rationalizing of the situation that he found his self in. The fragmented and almost montage-like quality of the film allegorizes the nature of Leonard’s mental condition.

Regarding Focal ontology, the non-linear narrative is encoded by means of the data property  focal:fabula . This term comes from the Russian Formalists Victor Shklovsky and Vladimir Propp, who were the first ones to carry an in-depth analysis on the distinction between:

  • fabula: the chronological order of the events contained in the story,
  • syuzhet: the linear progression of narrative from beginning to end.

The concept of syuzhet is pretty easy. Memento has 44 Sequences, 198 Scenes and a countless amount of Shots. In Focal, every individual belonging to the classes of Sequence, Scene and Shot are encoded with the chronological number as shown to the audience. This may sound difficult, but is very easy: e.g. the first scene is ♦Scene000, the last one is ♦Scene198. In the middle there will be, for example, a ♦Scene076, and after that a ♦Scene077, and after that a ♦Scene078, and so on. The number of each Scene represents its position inside the syuzhet of the movie.

Things get complicated when we start considering the fabula: not only Memento contains 18 flashbacks, embedded in the sequences, but also the color sequences, as written above, are shown in reverse order. By means of the data property  focal:fabula , it is possible to associate the Scene to an integer > 0, according to its chronological position in the order the movie.

For example: we have a chronologically ordered story of a girl who wakes up and goes to school, made of 5 Scenes with one Scene (no.4) being a flashback. The individuals and their data properties will be:

Scene#  focal:fabula   focal:content   focal:flashback   focal:diegetic_level 
Scene01 2 "the girl wakes up" False 0
Scene02 3 "the girl eats milk and cereal" False 0
Scene03 4 "the girl takes the bus" False 0
Scene04 1 "on the bus, the girl remembers when she was a child" True 1
Scene05 5 "the girl arrives at school" False 0

While the syuzhet is obviously 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, the fabula will be 2, 3, 4, 1, 5.

In the table, before, the notion of diegetic level has been introduced by means of the data property  focal:diegetic_level . Narrative levels (also referred to as diegetic levels) is an analytic notion whose purpose is to describe the relations among the plurality of narrating instances within a narrative, and more specifically the vertical relations between narrating instances/characters. In diegesis, the narrator tells the story. The narrator presents the actions (and sometimes thoughts, like in the aforementioned case of the little girl going to school) of the characters to the readers or audience.

The basic level of the Story contained in the Movie is 0. Level no. 1 means that it is a story within the movie, told by a character. If in the story, a new character starts telling another story, this latter one will have no. 2, and so on. In the case of a "breaking of the fourth wall", the level shall be -1.
